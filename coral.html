Coral Overview

Coral have three main categories: Small Polyp Stony (SPS), Large Polyp Stony (LPS), and Soft coral. Each of these have unique lighting and flow requirements, but generally have the same what chemistry requirements. The following sections will provide lighting arrangements and flow arrangements based on each type of coral. Some coral will differ from the norm and I will link to more information about them when they come up. I strongly recommend that you research more in depth the specific coral you are looking at before purchasing it to ensure you can provide the best environment for it. Flow is something you will have to experiment with, however, you can rent a tool called a par meter, to measure the light output in specific areas of the tank.

Small Polyp Stony (SPS)

SPS coral can be broken down into 2 categories: acropora, and not acropora. In general, acropora all of have the same lighting and flow requirements, whereas the rest of SPS have different requirements. In general these corals should be placed on or near the top of the rock structure in the tank so that they receive the correct flow and lighting conditions.

Acropora

The care requirements for acropora are that they need high light and high flow. High light is anything about 200 par and above. High flow will require some experimentation, however the key is that the flow should be turbulent and indirect. There should not be a pump pointed directly at a coral as this could damage it.

Non acropora

The care requirements for non acropora coral is much easier. They generally require medium lighting and medium flow. Medium light is anything between 125 and 175 par. Once again, flow will be something you will have to experiment with in order to get right, but it should be much gentler than for acropora.

Large Polyp Stony (LPS)

LPS corals have a fairly straight forward set of lighting and flow requirements. For the most part, these corals require low to medium light, and low to medium flow. Their par requirements range anywhere from 50 to 200 par depending on the coral. The placement of these corals should generally be in the middle to lower portion of the tank. However, if you are intentionally running your lights at a lower intensity, then you can keep these corals at the top of the rock scape.

Euphyllia and Fimbriaphyllia

These corals, called torch coral, frogspawn coral, and hammer coral, have similar care requirements. They require medium light in the 80 to 200 par. To keep them in higher light you must acclimate them over time by moving them higher up in the tank. They require medium to low indirect flow. These are corals that extend and flow and providing too much flow to them could damage their tissue.

Chalice and Favia/Favites

Chalice, Favia, and Favites corals all have a way of growing. They will grow and attach to the rock, which is called encrusting. These corals require medium or low light, 50 to 150 par, and medium to low flow. Again, we do not want to blow the flesh off of these corals, but enough to prevent debris from settling on them.

Soft Coral

Soft coral are much different from SPS and LPS corals. These corals do not have or grow a skeleton, hence their name soft corals. For the most part, these corals can thrive in a wide range of conditions. Most of these corals are grouped into a beginner friendly section as they are fairly hardy.

Zoanthids and Palythoas

Zoanthids and palythoas are very easy corals to keep. These corals can be kept in low to high light 50 to 200+ par, and in medium flow. They do not like to have lower flow because it allows debris and detritus to settle on their flesh. They can be placed on the rock work, or in the sand. It is best to keep them on a separated island or rock from the main rock structure to prevent them from growing too quickly. Please be careful and use gloves/eye protection when moving these corals because they can contain a potent neurological toxin. Use activated carbon in your filtration to neutralize any possible toxin in the water.

Mushrooms

Similar to zoanthid and palythoa corals, mushrooms can be kept in a fairly wide variety of conditions. However, these corals are a little more difficult to care for. They should be kept in low light, between 30 and 70 par, and should be kept in very low flow. Still low enough that detritus does not settle on them but not much more than that. These corals are similar to anemones in that they can 'walk' to find a place more suitable to them. However, they do so very very slowly and are more likely to detach from any substrate rather than move.
